SUMMARY
The current Debt feature proposal is a strong foundation. It is well-scoped, architecturally clean, and materially better than a generic balance tracker. The separation of concerns is especially strong:

- Debt = debt truth + debt math
- Bills = operational payment rows
- Overview = visibility layer
- Reporting = analysis layer
- Atlas = AI interpretation / recommendation layer

That structure is correct.

However, the current proposal is still stronger as a debt tracking system than as a debt outcome improvement system. It helps the user see debt better, but it does not yet provide enough decision-grade debt facts to reliably improve payoff strategy, early risk prevention, or cash-flow survivability.

BOTTOM-LINE ASSESSMENT
Current state:
- strong for debt organization
- strong for debt status visibility
- good for trust and system boundaries
- not yet strong enough for payoff optimization
- not yet strong enough for early risk prevention

WHAT IS STRONG
1) Debt is treated as a real truth + math engine
The proposal correctly treats Debt as more than a display layer. It stores debt account truth, calculates behavior, and exposes outputs such as balances, minimum or scheduled payments, payoff projection, installment progress, utilization, and account state.

2) Real-world debt states are recognized
The proposal accounts for states many debt trackers ignore, including:
- deferment
- no payment currently required
- no interest currently accruing
- interest accruing while no payment is required
- closed account with remaining balance
- behind / delinquent status

This is materially better than a simple list of balances.

3) The Debt / Bills boundary is clean
Debt owns debt truth and debt schedules. Bills owns near-term operational payment rows. That is the right split and avoids flooding the Bills workspace with a full lifetime payment schedule.

4) Trust and reliability language is good
The proposal explicitly says Debt should not fake certainty when key inputs are missing. That is correct for a trust-sensitive feature.

WHAT IS MISSING OR UNDERPOWERED
1) Extra-payment impact is not explicit enough
Debt should be able to show the objective consequence of paying more toward a given account.

Needed outputs:
- current payoff date
- payoff date with +$50 / +$100 / +$200
- months saved
- projected interest saved

This is not recommendation logic. It is debt truth that Atlas can use.

2) Credit card math is under-specified
The current design mentions utilization and interest visibility, but it does not clearly define support for:
- statement balance vs current balance
- grace period state
- daily accrual assumptions
- multiple APR buckets where relevant
- residual interest / trailing interest risk
- payment allocation behavior

Without that, card math may look correct while still being wrong enough to mislead users.

3) Delinquency is flagged, but consequences are not surfaced deeply enough
A delinquent label is not enough. Debt should expose structured facts such as:
- late fee risk
- promo failure risk
- grace-period loss risk
- collections escalation risk
- likely near-term consequences by debt type

4) Cash-flow survivability is too indirect
Debt should help expose whether the user can realistically stay current over the next 14 / 30 / 60 days.

Needed outputs:
- required debt cash in the next 14 / 30 / 60 days
- minimum cash needed to avoid going late
- due-date clustering risk
- paycheck-to-due-date mismatch signals for Atlas and Overview

5) Atlas needs more decision-grade data from Debt
Since Atlas is the AI financial advisor, Debt does not need to become a recommendation engine. But Atlas can only produce high-quality guidance if Debt exposes better structured facts.

Debt should provide Atlas with:
- extra-payment impact per account
- projected remaining interest
- daily or period interest cost where supported
- promo expiration facts
- risk flags tied to actual deadlines and balances
- required cash windows

RECOMMENDED V2 ADDITIONS
Highest-priority upgrades:

1. Extra payment scenario outputs
Per account, calculate:
- current payoff date
- payoff date with defined extra-payment increments
- interest saved
- months saved

2. Hard risk flags
Examples:
- promo expires within X days with balance remaining
- utilization above 80%
- interest accruing while no payment is required
- likely late within 30 days at current required-payment level

3. Credit-card-specific support
Add explicit modeling for:
- statement balance
- current balance
- grace period
- APR categories where relevant
- payment allocation behavior

4. Cash requirement windows
Expose:
- next 14 / 30 / 60 day required debt payments
- minimum needed to stay current
- concentration of obligations by date window

5. Verified vs estimated math state
For each account, clarify:
- what data is user-entered vs imported
- what data is missing
- whether payoff math is exact, limited, or estimated

INVESTMENT-HQ POSITION
From a risk-first and evidence-based perspective, the Debt feature is worth building. It is not fluff. It addresses a real financial need and gives the system a stronger source of debt truth.

But Debt should evolve from:
- "what debt do I have?"

to:
- "what exact financial consequence follows from each available move?"

That change is what will make Atlas materially more useful and what will improve real-world financial outcomes instead of only improving visibility.
