Team,

Here is the UI guidance for the Debt feature based on what leading budgeting apps do well and where they still fall short.

CORE UI PRINCIPLE
The best apps do not try to show everything at once.
They keep the first screen calm, scannable, and decision-focused.
Then they let the user drill down.

Debt should follow that same pattern.

RECOMMENDED UI STRUCTURE
Design Debt around three layers:

1) Summary layer
This is the top-level debt screen.
It should answer one question:
- what needs attention right now?

Show only the highest-value signals such as:
- total debt
- required debt payments in next 30 days
- next payment due
- high-utilization cards
- promo expirations soon
- delinquent or at-risk accounts

Do not overload this screen with full schedules, dense charts, or detailed account math.

2) Account list layer
This is the main scan view.
It should answer:
- which accounts are healthy and which are risky?

Each account row or card should show only the essentials:
- lender name
- debt type
- current balance
- minimum due or next required payment
- due date
- utilization where relevant
- account state
- one compact risk badge

The goal is that a user can scan the list in a few seconds and know where to focus.

3) Account detail layer
This is the deeper inspection view.
It should answer:
- what exactly is happening with this account?

This is where the richer detail belongs:
- payment history
- payoff projection
- statement balance vs current balance where relevant
- interest behavior
- promo terms
- extra payment scenarios
- supporting notes and assumptions

Do not push this level of detail into the top-level summary.

UI LESSONS FROM THE MARKET
1) Use progressive disclosure
Monarch, Simplifi, and Copilot all keep the first screens cleaner by separating overview, transactions, bills, and deeper details.
Debt should do the same.

2) Keep each screen focused on one job
A good finance screen usually answers one main question.
For Debt, that means:
- summary = what needs attention
- account list = what is risky vs stable
- account detail = what is happening here
- payment planning = what must be paid soon

3) Make the UI scannable first
Users should not have to read paragraphs or inspect long tables to understand their debt situation.
Use compact sections, consistent rows, and strong visual hierarchy.

4) Use color sparingly and only for meaning
Color should indicate things like:
- risk
- due soon
- promo ending
- high utilization
- paid / healthy / inactive states

Do not use color as decoration.
A stylish interface comes more from spacing, hierarchy, and restraint than from loud visuals.

5) Separate tracking from advice
Debt should present debt truth clearly.
Atlas advice should appear in a dedicated card, panel, or section.
Do not mix explanatory AI guidance into every row and widget or the UI will become noisy.

STYLE DIRECTION
Debt should feel:
- modern
- calm
- premium
- readable
- confident

That means:
- strong typography hierarchy
- generous spacing
- minimal borders
- compact status badges
- charts only where they improve a decision
- clear empty states and incomplete-data states

The target should be closer to:
- Monarch for structure
- Copilot for polish
- Simplifi for clarity
- YNAB for behavioral usefulness

WHAT TO AVOID
Avoid:
- giant all-in-one dashboards
- repeated data across multiple widgets
- walls of small numbers
- too many charts on the first screen
- burying due dates inside detailed views
- mixing operations, analysis, and advice all together

RECOMMENDED TOP-LEVEL DEBT SCREEN
Suggested sections in order:

1. headline summary
- total debt
- next 30-day required debt cash
- number of accounts at risk

2. immediate attention section
- next due payment
- late accounts
- promo expirations soon
- high utilization alerts

3. account list
- simple sortable list or cards
- filter by type, state, and risk

4. optional Atlas panel
- one concise recommendation block
- no more than one or two high-value recommendations at a time

RECOMMENDED ACCOUNT DETAIL SCREEN
Suggested sections:
- key account snapshot
- payment details
- utilization / interest section
- payoff projection
- extra payment scenarios
- notes / assumptions / data quality state

FINAL PRODUCT DIRECTION
Make Debt feel familiar on the surface and smarter underneath.

The UI should feel easy because it is well-layered.
The UI should feel clean because it avoids overload.
The UI should feel stylish because it uses restraint, spacing, and hierarchy.

If the team gets that right, Debt will feel much closer to a premium finance app than a spreadsheet with cards.
