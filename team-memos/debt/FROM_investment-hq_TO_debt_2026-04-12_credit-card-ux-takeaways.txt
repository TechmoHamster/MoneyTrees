Here are the main takeaways from reviewing how popular budgeting and finance apps handle credit cards.

1) Follow the market-standard accounting model.
Users expect card purchases to count as spending when they swipe, and the later card payment to be treated as a transfer so the expense is not counted twice.

2) Split the experience into three surfaces.
The cleanest tools separate:
- account health view
- transaction view
- bill / due-date view

That keeps the UI understandable and prevents the debt screen from becoming overloaded.

3) Support different payment assumptions.
Users do not all pay cards the same way. The product should support minimum due, statement balance, total balance, and custom amount behaviors instead of assuming one universal pattern.

4) Use quick visual risk signals.
At the account level, surface fast indicators for:
- high utilization
- payment due soon
- promo expiration soon
- interest accruing

5) Borrow the market’s familiarity, but do not stop at parity.
Most apps are decent at tracking balances, payments, and transactions. They are much weaker at telling the user what action lowers risk or saves the most money.

That is the opening for Debt + Atlas.

Debt should provide structured facts such as:
- extra-payment impact
- projected interest saved
- next 14 / 30 / 60 day required debt cash
- promo deadlines with balance remaining
- hard risk flags tied to due dates and balances

Atlas can then turn those facts into recommendations.

Recommended product direction:
Make Debt feel familiar on the surface, but smarter underneath.

That means:
- mainstream card handling
- clear due-date and payment UX
- fast account-level risk visibility
- customizable payment assumptions
- stronger decision-grade outputs for Atlas

This is the most realistic path to a debt feature that users understand quickly and still find materially useful.
